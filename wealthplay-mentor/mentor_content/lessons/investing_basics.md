# Lesson 1: What is Investing?

Investing means using your money to buy assets that grow in value over time. Instead of leaving money idle, investing helps it grow.

Example: If you invest ₹1,000 and it grows 10% in a year, it becomes ₹1,100.

---

# Lesson 2: Why Should Beginners Invest?

Investing early allows compounding to work. Compounding means your money earns returns, and those returns also earn returns.

Example: ₹5,000 invested monthly for 5 years at 10% annual return ≈ ₹3.9 lakh.

---

# Lesson 3: What Is a SIP?

SIP (Systematic Investment Plan) means investing a fixed amount regularly (weekly, monthly) instead of lump sum.

Benefits: Habit building, removes timing fear, smoother market ups/downs.

---

# Lesson 4: Emergency Fund Basics

Before investing, build an emergency fund: 3–6 months of expenses.

Purpose: safety net for medical, job loss, or urgent expenses.

---

# Lesson 5: Low-Risk vs High-Risk Investments

Low risk: FD, liquid funds  
Medium risk: Mutual Funds, SIPs  
High risk: Crypto, stocks, IPOs  

Beginners should start low to medium risk.

---

# Lesson 6: Diversification

Diversification means not putting money in one basket.

Example: Equity (40%), Debt (30%), Emergency Reserve (20%), Goals (10%).

---

# Lesson 7: Goal-Based Investing

Investing works best when tied to goals.

Examples:
- New phone in 6 months → Short term
- Education or travel → Mid term
- Retirement → Long term

---

# Lesson 8: Budgeting Formula

Simple rule: 50-30-20

- 50% needs
- 30% wants
- 20% save/invest

---

# Lesson 9: Risk Profile

Your investment choices depend on:
- Age
- Income stability
- Savings
- Emotional comfort

---

# Lesson 10: Common Beginner Mistakes

❌ Investing without emergency fund  
❌ Investing too much too fast  
❌ Copying others' portfolios  
❌ Reacting emotionally to market dips  

---

# Lesson 11: How to Start With ₹50,000 (Example Allocation)

- Emergency Fund → ₹15,000  
- SIP Mutual Fund → ₹20,000  
- Short Term Savings → ₹10,000  
- Learning Fund → ₹5,000  

---

# Lesson 12: What to Track

Track:
- SIP amount
- Portfolio growth
- Savings % increase
- Spending trends

Keep it simple — consistency matters more than perfection.
